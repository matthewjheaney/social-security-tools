No. If you look closely at the earnings table at the very top of this report, you'll notice that years further in the past have higher multipliers on your earnings than more recent years. Every year, the Social Security Administration adjusts all of these multipliers upwards to account for inflation.

The numbers in this report are showing the value in today's dollars. For example, if there is 10% inflation between now and the time you collect your benefit, then the actual benefit will be approximately 10% higher. In fact, your benefit will continue to grow to match inflation even in retirement. However, once you start taking your benefit, these increases will only be to match inflation, not a higher amount.

You may have heard that the Social Security program is running out of money. This is technically true, but only part of the story. Social Security is both paying out money to retirees and also collecting money from workers at the same time. Over it's 80-year history, the program has collected more money than it has paid out and has about a $3 trillion reserve of extra funds. However, in recent years the program is paying out more than it is collecting, and so the fund is shrinking.

Current estimates are that the fund will run out in 2037. At that time, the program will still be collecting money, and those collections will cover all but 21% of the current payments. So, if nothing at all changes by 2037, the numbers above will be reduced by 21% but the payments will still continue. Roughly speaking, your primary insurance amount of ${{recipient.primaryInsuranceAmount() | number:2}} would be reduced to: ${{recipient.primaryInsuranceAmount() * 0.79 | number:2}}. This is the 'worst case' scenario.

There are multiple mechanisms by which Congress could conceivably 'fix' the Social Security deficit, which would have less of an impact than the 'worst case' scenario. What will ultimately happen is unknown. Some of the ideas that have been proposed include:

  • Increase the payroll tax which funds the program.
  • Raise the ages at which benefits begin.
  • Change the method by which inflation adjustments are made to a more slowly rising value.
  • Change the bend points or ratios for the curve of annual indexed earnings.
  • Reduce the number of years from which benefits are calculated.
  • Reduce the spousal benefit.
  • Invest a percentage of the fund in equities.

It depends. You can see above how the age you choose affects your benefit amount over your lifetime. By delaying, you'll have a higher benefit when you do start taking it.

One consideration might be your health at the time of retirement. If you are unhealthy and unlikely to have many years of social security benefits, you may want to choose to begin benefits sooner. Conversely, if your health is excellent and you have reason to believe you will live to an old age, you may want to delay benefits and have higher amounts later in life.

Typically retirees have to worry more about finances the longer they live. Spending down savings over longer numbers of years increases the chance of running out or having to cut spending every year. Delaying Social Security benefits can be seen as a sort of insurance against the financial risk that living a long life brings.
